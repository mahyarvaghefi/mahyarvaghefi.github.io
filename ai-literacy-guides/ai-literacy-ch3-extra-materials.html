The Study Guide Problem

The Real Problem

Students skip materials that look like “more work” — even when the content would help them. It’s not a content problem. It’s a design problem.

What Students Ignore vs. What They Actually Use

✗ What Students IGNORE

• Dense paragraphs → They skip it

• No visual breaks → Feels overwhelming

• Looks like “more reading” → “I’ll get to it later” (never)

• No formatting → Looks like homework

• No clear structure → Where do I even start?

“Looks like a textbook threw up.”

✓ What Students Actually USE

• Clear sections with headers → They can scan

• Bullet points and short chunks → Not walls of text

• Visual elements → Boxes, icons, color

• Checklists they can mark off → Progress feels good

• Color-coding → Quick navigation

“Looks like a tool that’s there to help them.”

Design isn’t decoration. It’s whether they engage at all. Communication, not decoration.

The Five Components of an Effective Study Guide

1

Quick Overview

What: 3–5 bullet points summarizing the topic in under 100 words

Why: Gets them into the material without overwhelming — just enough to orient them.

2

Key Concepts

What: For each concept: Definition + Example + Common Mistake

Why: The “common mistake” piece is gold — students love knowing what trips people up.

3

Self-Check Questions

What: 5 questions students can ask themselves to test understanding — not quiz questions, thinking prompts

Examples: “Can I explain why this matters?” “Could I apply this to a new situation?”

4

Connections

What: How does this topic link to other course material?

Why: Students often miss these relationships — make them explicit.

5

Exam Tips

What: Specific advice for demonstrating mastery on YOUR particular assessment

Why: Generic study advice doesn’t help — your exam-specific tips do.

Discussion Questions That Actually Work

✗ Invites Short Answers

“What do you think about X?”

Problem: No cognitive demand. Students answer with a feeling, not thinking.

✓ Requires Thinking

“How would your answer change if Y happened?”

“Where does this logic break down?”

Result: Students can’t answer without actually thinking.

Discussion Questions at Different Cognitive Levels

Level Purpose Example Starter
Comprehension Check understanding of concepts “Explain in your own words…”
Application Use concepts in new situations “How would you apply this to…”
Analysis Break down and examine “What assumptions underlie…”
Evaluation Judge and defend positions “Which approach is stronger and why…”

Case Study Development

Case studies take forever to develop from scratch. AI can generate the framework — you add the relevance.

The AI + You Workflow for Case Studies
AI Provides

• Case study framework based on your concepts
• The structure and narrative arc
• Decision points and dilemmas
• Discussion prompts at different levels

You Add

• Local businesses students recognize
• Industry-specific examples from your expertise
• Current events that make it timely
• Details that make it relevant to YOUR students

Why This Works

AI handles the time-consuming structural work. You contribute what AI can’t: local context, industry knowledge, and understanding of what will resonate with your specific students.

The 4-Question Quality Filter

Critical reminder: For ANY AI-generated material — study guides, discussion questions, case studies — run it through this quality filter before you use it.

1

Does this reflect how your discipline actually organizes knowledge?

AI might structure something in a way that makes generic sense but misses how accounting, nursing, or history actually works.

2

Would a student in YOUR class find this helpful?

Not a generic student — your students. You know their backgrounds, their struggles, their context. AI doesn’t.

3

What do you know that AI couldn’t possibly know about this topic?

Your expertise, your experience, your judgment — these are irreplaceable. Add what only you can add.

4

Is anything technically wrong that sounds right?

AI can be confidently incorrect. It’s a specialty. Self-awareness doesn’t prevent the problem — verification does.

Extra Materials Checklist

Study Guides

5 components: Quick Overview → Key Concepts (def + example + mistake) → Self-Check Questions → Connections → Exam Tips

Discussion Questions

Require thinking, not just feeling. Generate at different cognitive levels. Avoid “What do you think about X?”

Case Studies

AI provides: framework, structure, decision points. You add: local businesses, industry examples, current events.

Quality Filter (All Materials)

4 Questions: Discipline conventions? Your students? Your expertise? Anything confidently wrong?

“AI generates the structure. You add the expertise. Always verify before using.”
