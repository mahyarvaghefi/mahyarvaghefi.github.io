How AI Learns, Fails, and Gets It Wrong

Lecture 4 Dr. Mahyar Sharif Vaghefi UT Arlington

1.7 — How AI “Learns”

Instead of programming every rule, AI systems are shown thousands (or billions) of examples. They find patterns in this data and use those patterns to generate responses.

The Cat Recognition Analogy

Think of teaching a child to recognize cats. You don’t write down every rule (“has whiskers, pointy ears, four legs…”). Instead, you show them hundreds of cat pictures until they learn to recognize the pattern themselves. That’s exactly how modern AI works.

What Was AI Trained On?

Large language models like ChatGPT and Claude were trained on massive amounts of text from the internet:

Wikipedia News Articles Academic Papers Reddit Threads Web Pages

“The good, the bad, and the questionable.”

Garbage In = Garbage Out

Training data quality matters enormously. If the data is biased, the AI is biased. If it’s outdated, the AI is outdated. If it’s wrong, the AI is wrong.

1.8 — AI Hallucinations

An AI “hallucination” is when the AI confidently generates information that sounds completely plausible — but is entirely false.

“Is That Like Lying?”

Not exactly. Lying implies knowing the truth and choosing to say something else. AI doesn’t know the difference between true and false — it just generates what sounds right based on patterns. Somehow, that’s worse.

Common Types of Hallucinations in Academia

Fake Citations
“According to Smith & Johnson (2023) in the Journal of Business Strategy…”

→ No such article exists
→ Authors may not exist
→ Journal might be real, but the article isn’t
→ The formatting is perfect

Fabricated Statistics
“Tesla’s Q3 2024 revenue was $45.2 billion, a 23% increase…”

→ Numbers sound plausible
→ Percentages seem reasonable
→ But completely made up

Invented Legal Cases
“In Johnson v. Amazon Corp (2022), the court established that…”

→ Case doesn’t exist
→ Can create detailed precedent
→ Sounds legally accurate
→ Lawyers have been sanctioned for citing AI-generated cases in court

False Company Information
“In 2021, Microsoft acquired Slack for $28 billion…”

→ Sounds plausible (Microsoft does acquisitions)
→ Price seems reasonable
→ Never happened (Salesforce bought Slack)

Remember

“I do everything with complete confidence. That’s part of the problem.”

1.9 — Two Types of AI You Must Understand

This is critical for business faculty. Not understanding this distinction can lead to confidently using hallucinated “current” data.

Knowledge Cutoff Models

What it is: AI trained on data up to a specific date, then “frozen.” They literally cannot know anything after that date.

Examples:

ChatGPT (base) Claude (base) Gemini (base)

Cutoff dates:
GPT-4: April 2023
GPT-4.5 / o1: Jan 2025
Claude: Varies by version

Real-Time Search Models

What it is: AI that can search the internet for current information, retrieve real data, and cite sources with links.

Examples:

ChatGPT + browsing Perplexity AI Gemini + search Claude + search

✓ Retrieves current web data
✓ Cites sources with links
✓ Updates with latest info

Critical: Same AI = Both Types!

The same AI can be both types depending on settings!

ChatGPT with browsing OFF → Knowledge cutoff model (stuck in the past)
ChatGPT with browsing ON → Real-time search model (can look things up)

Same product. Completely different capabilities. Always check if search/browsing is enabled!

What Each Type Can Handle

Task Type Knowledge Cutoff Search-Enabled
Current Stock Prices ✗ Will hallucinate or use old data ✓ Can retrieve real-time prices
Recent Company Earnings ✗ Limited to cutoff date ✓ Can access latest reports
Current Regulations ✗ May be outdated ✓ Can find recent changes
Today’s News ✗ Cannot access ✓ Can retrieve current news
Explaining Theories ✓ Excellent (Porter’s 5, NPV, etc.) ✓ Also good, may add sources
Business Frameworks ✓ Very good for established models ✓ Same, with sources
Creating Practice Problems ✓ Excellent at generating scenarios ✓ Equally good

1.10 — AI Bias: Automated Discrimination

AI learned from human-created content. Humans are biased. So AI inherited those biases — and sometimes amplified them.

Examples of AI Bias

Ask AI to describe a CEO → It might skew male
Ask AI about nurses → It might skew female
Ask AI for loan recommendations → It might favor certain demographics

Real Case Study: Apple Card

The Issue: Apple Card faced investigation for giving men higher credit limits than women with identical financial profiles.

What Happened: The algorithm wasn’t programmed to discriminate. But it learned from historical data where men had higher limits — and reproduced that pattern. Automatically. At scale.

The Takeaway: “So it automated the discrimination.” — Efficiently and invisibly. This happens in hiring systems, loan approvals, market analyses…

For Educators

Your students will use these systems professionally. They need to know how to question them.

1.11 — What ALL AI Struggles With

Even when AI isn’t hallucinating or showing bias, there are fundamental things it simply cannot do well.

Complex Calculations

AI can make arithmetic errors, especially in multi-step problems. Always verify quantitative analysis.

Ethical Judgment

AI can explain ethical frameworks, but it cannot make ethical decisions. That’s human work.

Contextual Nuance

AI misses organizational politics, cultural subtleties, and industry-specific norms.

True Creativity

AI recombines existing patterns. Breakthrough innovation still comes from humans.

Personal Experience

AI has no lived experience, emotions, or intuition from practice.

Strategic Wisdom

AI provides frameworks, but cannot replace executive judgment and leadership experience.

Key Takeaways

✗AI hallucinates — it confidently generates false citations, statistics, and facts.
✗Knowledge cutoff models can’t access current information — they may hallucinate “recent” data.
✗AI inherits human biases — and can automate discrimination at scale.
✗AI can’t do math reliably — always verify calculations.
✓Always check if search/browsing is enabled before trusting “current” information.
✓Always verify citations, statistics, and facts from AI outputs.
✓Teach students to question AI outputs — they’ll need this skill professionally.
