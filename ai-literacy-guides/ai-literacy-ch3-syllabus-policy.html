Zone 1: Course Preparation Overview

Course preparation is where you’ll see the biggest immediate payoff from AI. This zone covers everything you do before class begins.

Syllabus Development

Structure, learning objectives, pacing, AI policy statements

Course Materials

Slides, examples, chapter notes, study guides

Extra Materials

Discussion questions, debate prompts, case studies, advanced materials

Syllabus Development: Two Key Areas

AI can help with your syllabus in two distinct ways:

1

Reviewing & Improving Your Syllabus

Upload your existing syllabus and ask AI to check specific elements:

✓ Are learning objectives clear and measurable?

✓ Is the topic sequencing logical?

✓ Are any sections confusing to students?

✓ Do assessments align with objectives?

2

Writing Your AI Policy

The part you’ve been avoiding — creating clear guidelines for:

✓ How YOU (faculty) will use AI in the course

✓ How STUDENTS may use AI

✓ Documentation requirements

✓ Consequences for violations

Template Prompts

Template 1: Syllabus Improvement Focus
Role: You are an instructional design expert helping me improve my course syllabus.

Context: I teach a [undergraduate/graduate] [Course Name] course with [number] students. The course meets [frequency] for [duration]. [Describe any specific issues or goals]

Task: Review the attached syllabus. Please:
  1. Check if learning objectives are clear and measurable
  2. Identify any gaps in topic sequencing
  3. Flag any sections that might be confusing to students
  4. Suggest improvements to assignment descriptions

Constraint: Maintain my teaching voice and style. Suggest changes, don’t rewrite entirely.
Template 2: AACSB Alignment Focus
Role: You are an AACSB accreditation specialist and instructional design expert.

Context: I teach a [undergraduate/graduate] [Course Name] course at an AACSB-accredited business school. Our program-level learning goals are: [list your program’s learning goals]. We are preparing for [upcoming review type].

Task: Review the attached syllabus for AACSB alignment. Please:
  1. Map course learning objectives to program-level learning goals
  2. Verify learning objectives use measurable language (Bloom’s taxonomy)
  3. Check that assessments align with stated learning objectives
  4. Identify any gaps where learning goals are not addressed
  5. Suggest how to better document Assurance of Learning (AoL) measures

Constraint: Provide specific, actionable recommendations. Reference AACSB standards where applicable. Format output as a table mapping objectives → assessments → program goals.

Critical Constraint

“Maintain my teaching voice and style. Suggest changes, don’t rewrite entirely.”

AI audits. You decide what to change.

The 3-Question Check

Real AI output provides a useful framework but often has questionable details. Before accepting any AI suggestion, ask yourself:

1

Does this align with what I actually teach?

2

Would this make sense to my specific students?

3

Does this match my discipline’s conventions?

You’re not just accepting output — you’re filtering it.
Think of AI as an eager teaching assistant who’s smart but has never taken your class.

Your AI Policy: Two Essential Components

Your AI policy must address both sides of the classroom — your use AND student use.

Part 1: Faculty Disclosure (Your Use)

Why disclose? Transparency builds trust. When students know you use AI thoughtfully to improve their learning — not just to save yourself time — they respect it. It also models the behavior you want from them.

Sample Disclosure:

“I use AI to generate additional practice problems and varied examples. Some in-class content is AI-generated for critical analysis. All materials are reviewed for accuracy.”

Part 2: Student Policy (Their Use)

Be specific. Students need actual rules, not vibes.

✓ Which assignments allow AI assistance?

✓ Which assignments prohibit AI entirely?

✓ What documentation is required when AI is used?

✓ What are the consequences for violations?

Not a Policy

“Use AI responsibly” is NOT a policy. That’s a vibe. Students need actual rules.

Sample AI Policy (Complete Template)

Here’s a comprehensive AI policy template you can adapt for your course. Notice how it’s structured: specific, clear, and explains the “why.”

Course Policy on AI Use

At [Your Institution], we recognize that AI tools are becoming part of professional and academic life. This policy establishes clear expectations for how AI may — and may not — be used in this course.

✓ Permitted AI Use:

• AI tools may be used for brainstorming, generating initial ideas, or understanding concepts

• AI may be used to check grammar, clarity, or formatting of your own original work

• AI may be used for research assistance, such as finding sources or summarizing background info

• Any permitted use must be documented (see Documentation Requirements below)

✗ Prohibited AI Use:

• AI may not generate substantial portions of submitted work

• AI may not be used during exams or quizzes unless explicitly authorized

• AI-generated content may not be submitted as your own original thinking

• Using AI to complete work meant to assess your individual learning defeats the purpose

Documentation Requirements:

• When AI is used in any permitted capacity, you must disclose this in your submission

• Include: which tool you used, how you used it, and what portion reflects AI assistance

• Example: “I used ChatGPT to brainstorm initial outline ideas. The analysis and writing are my own.”

Why This Matters:

• Assignments are designed to build your skills and assess your understanding

• AI can help you learn, but it cannot learn for you

• Employers expect you to have the skills this course develops

Consequences for Policy Violations:

• Undisclosed or prohibited AI use will be treated as academic misconduct

• Even a first instance can result in a zero on the assignment or failure

• Severe or repeated violations may be referred to the Office of Student Conduct

The Principle: AI tools may be used to enhance your learning process, not replace it.
When in doubt, ask before submitting.

Making Your Policy Stick: The Acknowledgment Strategy

Here’s the problem: Students won’t read it. Page three of a document they skimmed for the refund deadline isn’t enough.

The Acknowledgment Strategy

Create a graded discussion post in the first week of class that requires students to confirm they’ve read and understood your AI policy specifically.

1

Create a graded discussion in Canvas (worth minimal points — 1%, but required)

2

Include the full AI policy text right in the discussion post — not linked, not in a PDF

3

Require students to post: “I read and understand the course AI policy and requirements.”

4

Set due date: End of Week 1. No exceptions.

Why This Works

Benefit How It Helps
Active Engagement Students must actively confirm — not passively scroll past
Documentation You have written evidence they acknowledged the policy
Unavoidable Policy is right there in the post — not buried in a PDF
Protection If misconduct occurs, you can point to their own acknowledgment

Detection Mechanism

The documentation requirement is also a detection mechanism. If writing quality is suspiciously high and there’s no AI disclosure… that’s a conversation starter.

Handling Student Objections

“Other professors let us use AI for everything.” — You’ll hear this. Here’s how to respond:

1. You’re Not Anti-AI — You’re Pro-Learning

Your policy reflects pedagogical choice, not technological fear. The “why” in your policy demonstrates this.

2. Different Courses Have Different Needs

An AI ethics course might encourage heavy AI use for analysis. A foundational writing course might restrict it more. Both are valid — as long as they’re clear.

3. You’re Explaining Pedagogical Choice

You’re not defending a universal rule. You’re explaining why THIS course has THESE expectations — and you made that transparent on day one.

Syllabus Prep Checklist

✓AI audits syllabus structure — you decide what to change
✓Use the 3-Question Check before accepting any AI suggestion
✓Disclose your AI use to build trust with students
✓Write specific AI policies — permitted uses, prohibited uses, documentation, consequences
✓Require week-one discussion post acknowledging AI policy
✓Explain why the policy exists — not just what the rules are

“An hour of policy work now saves hours of ‘I didn’t know AI wasn’t allowed’ conversations in week ten.”
