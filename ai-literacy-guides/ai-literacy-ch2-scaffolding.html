Chapter 2: Scaffolding — Don’t Ask for Everything at Once

Lecture 7 Dr. Mahyar Sharif Vaghefi UT Arlington

2.5 — The Problem: Asking for Everything at Once

When you need something complex — a full assignment with instructions, rubric, and sample solutions — it’s tempting to ask for it all in one giant prompt. This is a common mistake.

Why “Everything at Once” Fails

If you ask AI for everything at once, it tries to do it all — and does none of it well.

It’s like asking someone to write a novel, design the cover, and plan the marketing campaign all in one breath.

The Solution: Scaffolding

Break complex tasks into a conversation. Build step by step, refining as you go. Think of it like construction scaffolding — you build up to the final product layer by layer.

2.6 — Four Scaffolding Approaches

Different tasks call for different scaffolding strategies. Here are four powerful approaches:

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

What it is: Ask AI to “think step-by-step” or “show your reasoning” at each stage.

Best for: Complex analytical tasks, problem-solving, multi-step calculations.

Example: “Explain how to calculate WACC. Think step-by-step and show your reasoning at each stage.”

Why it works: AI slows down, works through each component, and you can catch errors along the way.

Template-Based Prompting

What it is: Provide a structure or template you already like and have AI fill it in.

Best for: Standardized documents, consistent formatting, reusable structures.

Example: “Use this rubric template and create criteria for a finance presentation: [paste your template]”

Why it works: You can reuse formats that already work for you and your institution.

Role-Playing Scenarios

What it is: Have AI simulate different perspectives or argue from specific viewpoints.

Best for: Case discussions, ethical dilemmas, seeing multiple angles, debate prep.

Example: “Take the role of a skeptical CFO. How would you challenge this investment proposal?”

Why it works: Helps students see multiple angles. You can have AI argue both sides of a business decision.

Iterative Refinement ★ Most Powerful

What it is: Start broad, then refine with follow-up prompts. Build the final product through a series of focused exchanges.

Best for: Creative tasks, content development, complex multi-part assignments.

Example Process: First get outline → Then develop each section → Then refine tone → Then create rubric

Why it works: You catch problems early — before they compound. AI’s first answer is rarely its best answer.

2.7 — Real Example: Building a Marketing Campaign Project

Let’s see iterative refinement in action. Goal: Create a complete marketing campaign project for students with instructions, rubric, and detailed criteria.

1. Structure
→
2. Instructions
→
3. Rubric
→
4. Refine
1

Getting the Structure

Prompt: “You are a marketing professor. I need to create a group project where students develop a complete marketing campaign for a new product launch. The project should take 3–4 weeks and involve market research, target audience analysis, and campaign strategy. First, help me outline the major components this project should include. What are the key sections students should complete?”

Purpose: Get the skeleton first. Review it, make sure you like the direction, then go deeper.

2

Developing Instructions

Prompt: “Great! Now let’s develop detailed instructions for the ‘Target Audience Analysis’ section. Students should identify and profile their target customers using demographic, psychographic, and behavioral data. Write clear instructions that specify: What data points to include (age, income, lifestyle, values, buying behavior) — Where to find this data (industry reports, surveys, census data) — How to create a detailed customer persona (with example format) — Expected length (about 2–3 pages). Write this as if you’re speaking directly to students.”

Purpose: Build one piece at a time. If you don’t like this section, fix it now — before building more on a flawed foundation.

3

Creating the Rubric

Prompt: “Now create a grading rubric for the entire project. Use a 100-point scale distributed as: Market Analysis: 20 points — Target Audience Analysis: 20 points — Campaign Strategy: 25 points — Creative Execution: 20 points — Budget & Metrics: 15 points. For each section, define criteria for Excellent (90–100%), Good (80–89%), Satisfactory (70–79%), and Needs Improvement (below 70%).”

Purpose: Still guiding, still specific. You’re telling AI the categories and point distribution.

4

Refining Based on Feedback

Prompt: “The rubric looks good, but I want the ‘Excellent’ criteria for Campaign Strategy to be more specific. Students should not just describe a strategy — they need to: 1. Clearly define campaign objectives (SMART goals) 2. Explain the strategic rationale (why this approach?) 3. Connect strategy to target audience insights 4. Differentiate from competitor campaigns. Rewrite just the ‘Campaign Strategy — Excellent’ criteria to reflect these expectations.”

Purpose: Surgical edit. Instead of scrapping everything and starting over, you refine just the part that needs work.

2.8 — The Key Insight

Remember

“AI’s first answer is rarely its best answer.” Iteration gets you to quality.

✗ “Everything at Once” ✓ Scaffolding Approach
Feels faster initially Feels slower initially
Usually leads to 3+ rounds of “no, that’s not what I wanted” Problems caught early, before they compound
Errors build on errors Each piece reviewed before the next is built
Actually slower overall Actually faster overall

Which Approach When?

Approach Best For Example Use Cases
Chain-of-Thought Complex analytical tasks, problem-solving WACC calculations, financial analysis, multi-step problems
Template-Based Standardized documents, consistent formatting Syllabi, rubrics, assessment templates
Role-Playing Case discussions, ethical dilemmas CFO perspective, regulator viewpoint, debate prep
Iterative Refinement Creative tasks, content development Full assignments, projects, comprehensive materials

Key Takeaways

✓Don’t ask for everything at once — AI tries to do it all and does none of it well.
✓Four scaffolding approaches: Chain-of-Thought, Template-Based, Role-Playing, and Iterative Refinement.
✓Iterative Refinement is most powerful — get the skeleton first, then build and refine piece by piece.
✓Catch problems early — fix a section before building more on a flawed foundation.
✓Scaffolding feels slower but is faster — it avoids multiple rounds of “that’s not what I wanted.”

“Four focused prompts instead of one messy one — and you end up with exactly what you need.”
