Lecture 10 Dr. Mahyar Sharif Vaghefi UT Arlington

Five Mistakes to Avoid

Even with the Four Pillars framework, there are common pitfalls that trip up even experienced AI users. Here are five mistakes we’ve all made — and how to avoid them.

Good News

About 80% of first-time prompts make at least one of these mistakes. Once you know what to watch for, you’ll avoid them naturally.

1

The “Vague Assignment” Prompt

Using vague statements to describe your task

✗ The Mistake

“Create a case study for my finance class.”

No course level. No topic. No length. No learning objectives. Nothing about what makes this YOUR class.

It’s like ordering “food” at a restaurant. You’ll get something — might be a salad, might be a whole roast chicken. Probably not what you had in mind.

✓ The Fix

“Create a case study for my undergraduate corporate finance course, focused on capital budgeting decisions, approximately 800 words, with three discussion questions that require students to apply NPV analysis.”

2

The “One-and-Done” Approach

Accepting the first output without iteration

✗ The Mistake

You get a response and just… accept it. No feedback, no refinement, no back-and-forth.

AI’s first draft is like a paper from an enthusiastic graduate student who wrote it at 2 AM. It’s fine — but it’s rarely the best work.

You wouldn’t just slap an A on that paper and move on. You’d give feedback.

✓ The Fix: Give Feedback!

• “This is close, but make the scenarios more challenging.”
• “Good structure, but can you tighten up the second section?”
• “I like the direction — now make it harder to game.”

Remember: AI is a collaborator, not a vending machine. Second and third versions are almost always stronger.

3

The “Missing Guardrails”

Only telling AI what you want, not what you DON’T want

✗ The Mistake

“Create practice problems for statistics.”

But: Do you want calculus-based problems? Should AI avoid simplified textbook examples? Are there topics you haven’t covered yet?

⚠ Real Example

Someone asked for exam questions — great prompt, very detailed. AI generated questions. Half of them used a method they explicitly weren’t teaching that semester.

✓ The Fix: Add Exclusions

“Take Main Street” is helpful. “Take Main Street, but not during rush hour and avoid the construction on 5th” is better.

Include things like: “Avoid calculus-based approaches.” “Don’t use topics from Chapter 8 onwards.” “No problems that require software.”

4

The “Kitchen Sink”

Asking for everything at once

✗ The Mistake

“Create an assignment with detailed instructions, a grading rubric, a sample solution, aligned learning objectives, discussion questions, and maybe a participation component.”

That’s six deliverables in one prompt. AI will attempt all of them. AI will nail none of them.

✓ The Fix: Use Scaffolding

Break it into steps. Each piece builds on the last, and you can catch problems before they cascade.

1. Assignment draft → 2. Refine it → 3. Create rubric → 4. Sample solution

It’s slower upfront, but faster overall. Like building anything — foundation first.

5

The “Trust Fall” ⚠ Most Important

Not verifying factual claims

AI’s Honest Confession

“I generate plausible-sounding information. Citations. Statistics. Historical claims. Names. Dates. Sometimes they’re accurate. Sometimes they are entirely made up.”

“And here’s the uncomfortable part: I genuinely cannot tell the difference.”

How Hallucinations Work

AI doesn’t hallucinate obviously. It doesn’t say “the moon is made of cheese.” Instead, it says things like:

“According to a 2019 study in the Journal of Applied Economics…”

The journal is real. The claim sounds reasonable. But the citation? Potentially fabricated.

Non-Negotiable Rule

Every citation. Every statistic. Every specific factual claim.
Verify it. Every. Single. Time.

✓ Use AI For:

Drafting Brainstorming Structuring Iterating Creative work

✗ Use YOUR Expertise + Google Scholar For:

Fact-checking Citation verification Statistical accuracy

Quick Recap: The Five Mistakes

# Mistake The Problem The Fix
1 Vague Prompts “Create a case study.” Add context, constraints, and specifics
2 One-and-Done Accepting first output Iterate — give feedback, refine
3 Missing Guardrails No exclusions specified Tell AI what NOT to do
4 Kitchen Sink Asking for everything at once Break into steps (scaffolding)
5 Trust Fall ⚠ Not verifying facts/citations ALWAYS verify factual claims

Key Takeaways

✓Be specific, not vague — context, constraints, and details make all the difference.
✓Iterate, don’t one-and-done — AI is a collaborator, not a vending machine.
✓Tell AI what to avoid — exclusions matter as much as inclusions.
✓Break big asks into steps — scaffolding is slower upfront but faster overall.
!ALWAYS verify the facts — AI generates plausible-sounding information that may be fabricated.

“Do those five things, and we’re going to work very well together.”
