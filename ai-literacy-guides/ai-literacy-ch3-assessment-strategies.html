Chapter 3, Zone 3: Assessment Strategies for the AI Era

Lecture 19 Dr. Mahyar Sharif Vaghefi UT Arlington

The Assessment Challenge, Reframed

The Key Insight

AI doesn’t threaten assessment. It forces us to be BETTER at assessment.

Old Mindset vs. New Mindset

✗ Old Mindset: Prevention Focus

“How do I prevent students from using AI to cheat?”

Problem: Detection tools produce false positives that harm innocent students and false negatives that miss actual AI use. You’re entering an arms race you can’t win.

✓ New Mindset: Better Design Focus

“How do I design assessments that measure what actually matters, with or without AI?”

Result: Students learn more. Assessments are more authentic. AI becomes either irrelevant to the assessment or a helpful tool within it.

Detection Tools Are Not Reliable

AI detection tools produce false positives that can harm innocent students and false negatives that miss actual AI use. Building your assessment strategy around detection is building on sand.

Your AI Policy: The Foundation

Remember the AI policy you created during syllabus development? Students signed an acknowledgment in Week One. That policy now becomes your foundation for assessment integrity.

How Your Policy Does the Heavy Lifting

Clear expectations from day one: Students agreed to permitted uses, prohibited uses, and documentation requirements.

Transforms difficult conversations: Instead of “I think you used AI,” you say “Let’s discuss whether your submission aligns with the policy you agreed to.”

Creates accountability without forensics: You’re asking students to demonstrate their process matched their commitment.

Questions Your Policy Enables

Did they document what they were supposed to? Can they explain their work in an oral discussion? Does their draft progression make sense?

The policy creates accountability. The assessment design creates evidence. Together, they make integrity conversations much easier.

Five Assessment Strategies for the AI Era

Strategy 1: Add Process Documentation

Principle: Require students to show their work, not just their final product.

• Require draft submissions (outline, rough draft, revision)

• Ask for reflection on process — how did they approach it? What changed?

• If AI permitted, require documentation: prompts used, how output was evaluated and modified

Key insight: If someone submits a polished product with no evidence of process, that’s a conversation worth having — regardless of whether AI was involved.

Strategy 2: Require Personal Connection

Principle: Design assignments requiring specific personal experience, context, or data AI doesn’t have access to.

• Analyze their own company’s situation

• Interview practitioners in their field

• Use data they collect themselves

Key insight: AI can’t help because AI doesn’t know what they know. The substance has to come from them.

Strategy 3: Emphasize Oral Defense

Principle: Add verbal components where students must explain and defend their work.

• 2-minute presentation walking through their analysis

• Q&A about their submission: “Why this choice? What alternatives?”

• Question bank method: anyone might be called to explain any part of group work

Key insight: If someone used AI to generate work they don’t understand, this becomes immediately apparent.

Trade-off: Takes class time. Worth it for high-stakes assignments; maybe not for low-stakes practice.

Strategy 4: Make AI Part of It

Principle: Instead of prohibiting AI, require its use in structured ways that develop critical skills.

✗ Old: “Write an analysis”

✓ New: “Use AI to generate an initial analysis, then critique it, identify what’s missing or wrong, and produce an improved version with your additions”

Now assessing: Their judgment, their ability to evaluate AI output, their expertise in knowing what AI got wrong.

Key insight: No cheating concern because AI use IS the assignment. The question is whether they used it thoughtfully.

Strategy 5: The Two-Version Method

Principle: Require students to submit two versions — one without AI, one with AI enhancement.

Version 1: Their Own Work (No AI)

Their baseline — what they can do independently. They MUST do the work first.

Version 2: AI-Enhanced

After submitting V1, they use AI to review, critique, and improve. Submit with every prompt used and a reflection on what AI caught that they missed.

Sample Grading Weight:

• Version 1 (independent skill): 60%

• Improvement between versions (growth): 20%

• Quality of prompts and reflection (AI literacy): 20%

Key insight: Students see patterns in their own weaknesses. “AI keeps catching my calculation errors.” Two skills for the price of one assignment.

The Assignment Audit Framework

Before redesigning every assignment, apply these three tests to evaluate what you already have:

1

The AI Test

Question: Could a student get a passing grade by putting your assignment prompt directly into AI?

If yes: Redesign needed.

2

The Value Test

Question: Does this assess something that matters in careers, even with AI available?

If the skill becomes irrelevant when AI can do it: Maybe the assignment should be replaced entirely.

3

The Process Test

Question: Is the learning in the product or in the thinking process?

If it’s all in the product: Add process elements — drafts, reflection, oral explanation, or the two-version method.

Start with your highest-stakes assessments. Apply the three tests. Redesign what needs it.

AI-Assisted Grading: Appropriate vs. Inappropriate

With 150 students and grading taking an entire weekend, can AI help? Yes — but with clear boundaries. AI can assist your judgment, never replace your judgment.

✓ Appropriate AI Uses

• Creating comprehensive rubrics with clear criteria and performance levels

• Checking your grading consistency across all papers

• Drafting feedback comments that you review and personalize

• Identifying common errors across submissions to know what to reteach

• Flagging submissions that need extra attention

✗ Inappropriate AI Uses

• Assigning final grades without human review

• Using AI-generated scores without verification

• Automating all feedback with no personal touch

• Trying to “detect” AI use in student work — unreliable and damages trust

• Uploading student names or identifying information to AI tools

The Bottom Line

AI helps you be more consistent and thorough, but you make the final calls. You remain responsible for assessment decisions. AI is a tool, not a replacement for professional judgment.

Template Prompt: Rubric Development

Most rubrics are either too vague (“demonstrates understanding”) or too rigid to capture the range of student work. AI can help you create better rubrics faster.

Template: Rubric Development
Role: You are an assessment expert helping me create a grading rubric.

Context: I’m creating a rubric for [assignment type] in my [Course Name] course for [student level]. The assignment asks students to: [describe assignment]

Learning objectives being assessed:
  1. [Objective 1]
  2. [Objective 2]

Task: Create a detailed rubric with:
  • [Number] main criteria categories
  • Point distribution: [list categories and points]
  • [Number] performance levels (Excellent/Good/Satisfactory/Needs Improvement)

Requirements:
  • Criteria must be specific and observable
  • Each level clearly distinguishable
  • Student-facing language
  • Focus on learning objectives, not just formatting

Time Savings

Instead of staring at a blank rubric template for an hour, you have a draft in two minutes that you spend fifteen minutes refining. AI handles the tedious structural work. You add the expertise and judgment.
