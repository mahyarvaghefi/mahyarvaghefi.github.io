2.1 — The Problem: Same Tool, Different Results

Have you ever used ChatGPT or Claude and received disappointing results, while a colleague using the exact same tool gets impressive, professional outputs? The difference isn’t intelligence or technical skill — it’s knowing how to communicate with AI.

The Key Insight

Telling a teaching assistant “make me some questions” gets you garbage. Explaining exactly what you need — course level, topics, difficulty, format — gets you something useful. AI works the same way.

The Good News

There’s a framework. Once you learn it, you’ll get consistent results too.

2.2 — The Four Pillars of Effective Prompts

Think of a good prompt like a well-designed assignment you’d give students. It has clear expectations, context, constraints, and a defined output. Here are the four essential components:

1

Role / Persona

What it is: Tell the AI who it should “be” or what perspective to take.

Why it matters: This frames the AI’s knowledge and tone. Like giving an actor a character before they perform.

Example: “You are an experienced finance professor teaching undergraduates…”
2

Context

What it is: Background information the AI needs to understand your situation.

Why it matters: Context sets the boundaries of what AI can assume about your students’ knowledge level.

Example: “I’m teaching a 300-level corporate finance course. Students have covered time value of money and basic valuation.”
3

Task / Instruction

What it is: What you actually want done — your core request.

Why it matters: The more specific your task, the less AI has to guess. And AI guesses are confident but often wrong.

Vague: “Create three practice problems.”

Better: “Create three practice problems on capital budgeting using NPV analysis, each requiring students to compare two investment alternatives.”
4

Constraints / Format

What it is: Length, style, structure, and what to avoid.

Why it matters: Constraints work both ways — what you want AND what you don’t want.

Example: “Each problem should be 150–200 words. Include realistic company names. Provide answer keys separately. Avoid problems requiring calculus.”

The Four Pillars at a Glance

Pillar Question It Answers What to Include
1. Role “Who should I be?” Expert persona, professional role, perspective to take
2. Context “What’s the situation?” Course level, student background, topics covered, learning objectives
3. Task “What should I do?” Specific action, number of items, type of output, specific topics
4. Constraints “What are the limits?” Word count, format, difficulty level, what to avoid, answer key needs

2.3 — Think of AI Like a Teaching Assistant

The Teaching Assistant Analogy

AI is like a highly capable but extremely literal assistant. It has vast knowledge but needs clear direction to apply it appropriately.

Vague vs. Clear Requests

✗ Vague to TA: “Make me some questions.”
✓ Clear to TA: “Create 5 multiple-choice questions on capital budgeting for my junior-level finance students who have covered NPV. Medium difficulty, 4 answer choices each, include answer key.”

2.4 — Try It Yourself: Before and After

Use this template to transform your prompts. Fill in each pillar with details specific to your need:

✗ Before (Weak Prompt)

“Make me a multiple choice finance question.”

✓ After (Strong Prompt Using Four Pillars)

Role You are an experienced finance professor creating exam materials.

Context I teach FINA 3317 (Financial Institutions and Markets) to undergraduate juniors. Students have completed modules on interest rate determination, bond markets, and financial intermediaries.

Task Create 5 multiple-choice questions that test application-level understanding of how financial institutions operate and how interest rates respond to economic conditions.

Constraints Each question must have 4 answer choices. Include realistic financial market numbers. Difficulty: Medium. Provide answer keys separately.

Key Takeaways

✓Same tool, different results — the difference is how you ask, not the AI itself.
✓Four Pillars: Role, Context, Task, Constraints — get these right for dramatic improvement.
✓Be specific: The more detail you provide, the less AI has to guess.
✓Constraints include exclusions: Tell AI what NOT to do, not just what to do.
✓It’s a learnable skill: Prompt engineering isn’t about intelligence — it’s about knowing the formula.

“Your colleague isn’t smarter — they just know the formula. Now you know it too.”
