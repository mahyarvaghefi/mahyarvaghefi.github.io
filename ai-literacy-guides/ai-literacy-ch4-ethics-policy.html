The Policy Landscape: Guardrails, Not Walls

You might want someone to just tell you the rules. The reality? Most institutions have guidance, recommendations, and frameworks — but not binding policies. You’re navigating ethical terrain with guardrails, not walls.

UTA’s Approach: Guidance Over Mandates

UTA’s GenAI Guidelines for Instruction frame AI integration around student learning outcomes rather than blanket permissions or prohibitions. Faculty are asked to think about what serves their students, not just follow rules.

The Key Shift

You’re being asked to think about what serves your students, not just follow rules. Professional judgment, not compliance checklists.

Three Transparency Principles

✓

Fact-Check Everything

Verify AI-generated content before using or sharing it

✓

Disclose AI Use

Be transparent about when and how you’re using AI in your teaching

✓

Communicate Expectations in Your Syllabus

Clear AI policies for both your use and student use from day one

Data Privacy: The Hard Line

While most policy areas offer flexibility, data privacy is where institutions draw a hard line.

UTA’s Data Classification Requirement

GenAI tools may only be used with data classified as “Public.” All content entered into, or generated by, a public GenAI tool are available to the public.

Understanding AI Tool Types

Understanding the distinction between public AI tools and institutionally-approved platforms is essential for data privacy and protection. Your choice of tool has direct implications for data security.

The EDUCAUSE Framework: Eight Foundational Principles

The EDUCAUSE working group developed a comprehensive framework that provides the ethical foundation for AI in education.

1. Beneficence

Enhance learning, minimize harm

2. Justice

Equitable access, fair outcomes

3. Respect for Autonomy

Informed choice for faculty and students

4. Transparency

Clear information about how AI operates

5. Accountability

Humans retain responsibility for outcomes

6. Privacy Protection

Safeguard personal information

7. Nondiscrimination

Prevent algorithmic bias

8. Continuous Evaluation

Monitor and refine practices

Three Principles That Trip Faculty Up Most

While all eight principles matter, three tend to cause the most problems in practice:

1. Accountability: If AI Messes Up, It’s Still Your Fault

AI can’t be held responsible — it’s a tool. If AI-generated content contains errors, if AI-assisted grading is unfair, if AI feedback harms a student — you’re accountable.

The responsibility never transfers to the technology.
2. Justice: Your Tool Choices Have Equity Implications

If you require AI tools, are you creating access barriers?

Access Gap: Premium AI tools are behind paywalls. Students from lower-income backgrounds may only access inferior free versions.
Detection Bias: Evidence shows AI detection tools disproportionately flag non-native English speakers.
Always ask: Who benefits? Who might be disadvantaged?
3. Privacy: Student Work = Student Intellectual Property

Students own the texts they produce. Entering their work into AI systems potentially transfers their intellectual property to commercial entities.

Essential Safeguards: Never enter sensitive data into public AI tools. Use institutionally-approved platforms. Anonymize student work if you must use public tools.

High-Risk Data (Never Upload to Public AI): Personal narratives • Recommendations • Accommodation information • Grading data • Student names/identifiers

Six Practical Principles You Can Apply Today

1

Ethical Frameworks Require Practical Wisdom

The rules won’t cover everything — you’ll have to think through novel situations using your professional judgment.

2

Transparency Should Be Bidirectional

Don’t demand what you won’t practice yourself. If you expect students to disclose AI use, you should disclose yours.

3

Grading Use Demands Caution

Objective metrics only; human judgment for subjective evaluation. Always disclose to students when AI assists in grading.

4

Equity Requires Proactive Design

Consider access barriers before requiring tools. Don’t discover equity issues after they’ve harmed students.

5

Privacy Is Non-Negotiable

No student data in public AI tools. Ever. This is not a guideline — it’s a hard line.

6

The Goal Is Human-AI Collaboration

AI augments your judgment; it never replaces it. You remain the final interpreter of meaning.
